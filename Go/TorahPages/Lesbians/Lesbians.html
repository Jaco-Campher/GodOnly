Torah

Let’s start with Torah, because If we are going to call something sin, we need to show where it says so in the Torah.

The word mankind is male and the word for womankind is woman or wife. We don’t get a clear answer of whether the word “Thou” is a man or a woman, as it is part of the translation and there is no strong number for it. So, we will simply test both to be safe.

If we replace “Thou” with man or male, it will read:

“A man shalt not lie with mankind, as with womankind: it is abomination.”

This matches with what we find in Leviticus 20.

This clearly shows that it is a sin for two men to sleep together. But as you can see from Lev 20:13, it does not address women sleeping together.


So, let’s go back to Lev 18:22 and see what it says when we replace the word “Thou” with woman.

“A woman shalt not lie with mankind, as with womankind: it is abomination.”

Now here we run into a problem, read the first part again.

“A woman shalt not lie with mankind,”

This would mean that a woman cannot sleep with a man, so basically the end of humanity. This would also contradict other verses in the Bible.


If we assume that “Thou” is a woman, then the verse also contradicts itself. It becomes nonsensical, saying that a woman shall not lie with a man as with a woman. Clearly the “Thou” in Lev 18:22 cannot be a woman.

These are the two main verses I could find in Torah that directly talks about same sex relationships. And as you can see, they both apply only to men sleeping together.

The First Possible Exception

In the introduction I said that there are two possible exceptions, and the first one is also found in Torah. But it is not what you might expect:

Here again as before we don’t get a clear indicator if “Thou” is a man or a woman. So again, we will test both.

First, we will make “Thou” a man as before.

“Neither shalt a man take a wife to her sister, to vex her, to uncover her nakedness, beside the other in her life time.”

This is pretty much what you would expect, and I think the way that most people understand it.


But now look what happens when we make “Thou” a woman.

“Neither shalt a woman take a wife to her sister, to vex her, to uncover her nakedness, beside the other in her life time.”

Do you see it?

For a woman to take a wife to her sister, we must assume that the first two women are already married.

Lev 18:18 seems to work with “Thou” being a man or a woman as there is no contradictions as in Lev 18:22, other than our preconceived ideas perhaps. But what about Lev 18:22 where we saw that “Thou” can only be a man?

This is sort of a catch-22 situation, if “Thou” in Leviticus 18 can only be a man, then we a back to square one and there are no laws in Torah against women sleeping together. If we assume that “Thou” can be a woman, then there is support for two women marrying each other.

In either case, none of these laws would prevent women from sleeping together or marrying each other. So where does the idea come from that it is a sin?
